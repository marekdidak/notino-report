Target personas
B2C
Urban digital beauty seeker

Mid-sized hair/nail/aesthetic clinic with multiple staff. Wants full ops OS: POS, inventory, HR, analytics. Will compare Notino against Salonkee on feature depth before switching.

Notino Partner — competitive position summary
Where Notino wins outright
Unique ecosystem — e-commerce + bookings + loyalty. No competitor replicates this.
True zero cost — free for active salons (20+ bookings/month). Not "free with asterisks" like Fresha.
5% product commission — turns a cost centre into a revenue source. Unique in the market.
2.4M existing AT users — no booking tool has this consumer base to leverage for demand generation.
Where Notino must improve
AT B2B brand awareness for Notino Partner is near zero — must build fast before Planity arrives.
Ops feature depth (HR, payroll export, advanced reporting) lags Salonkee and Time Globe for larger salons.
500–800 AT salon target is modest vs Treatwell's 2,000 — acceleration needed, especially in Vienna.

1. Treatwell
Treatwell
B2C dominantB2B weak
Channels: Instagram @treatwell_dach (DACH) · Facebook 213k fans @treatwelldach · App-store presence · In-house performance marketing team
"Der schönste Weg, um Friseur-, Beauty- und Wellness-Termine zu buchen." / "Treat yourself well. It's a non-negotiable." — bio lines across DACH channels
Pillar 1
Self-care as identity — beauty isn't a luxury, it's self-respect. Emotional permission to book.
Pillar 2
"Top Rated" awards — seasonal salon highlights, 5-year winners, credibility stacking.
Pillar 3
Discovery / inspiration — trending beauty looks, seasonal themes (Christmas, weddings, summer). Drives impulse bookings.
Pillar 4
Social proof loops — "real review" carousels, before/after UGC, salon spotlights to build trust for new bookers.
Pillar 5 (ads)
Facebook App Install Ads + retargeting. "Book now" CTA. Seasonal promotions. No mention of salon economics.
Tone
Warm, aspirational, slightly playful. Talks to the consumer as someone who deserves care. Never talks money or commissions publicly.
Core hook
"The easiest way to find and book beauty." Convenience + discovery + credibility. "Europe's #1" used constantly as a trust shortcut.
What they never say
The 35% commission, marketplace dependency, or that salons' regular clients also go through Treatwell's system at cost. B2B content is minimal and defensive.
Notino's opening
Treatwell's B2B social silence is a real gap. Salon owners on social hear Treatwell only as a consumer brand — not as a business partner. Notino can own the "salon owner voice" channel entirely in AT.
2. Fresha
Fresha
B2C + B2B
Channels: Instagram 73k (@fresha) · LinkedIn (active, case studies) · TikTok · YouTube · Blog. Global English-first.
"Connecting the world to beauty and wellness." / "Supercharge your business with the world's top booking platform." — consistent cross-channel taglines
Pillar 1
Salon owner empowerment — "Meet the Partner" video series. Real business owners, real revenue uplift. Emotional + aspirational.
Pillar 2
"Free software" narrative — historically dominant. Now quietly shifting to "affordable" and "value" as pricing changed.
Pillar 3
Product feature drops — 2026 roadmap (AI receptionist, two-way messaging, bundles) heavily promoted. Innovation posture.
Pillar 4
Social media as booking lever — heavily pushes "Book Now" on Instagram/Facebook/Google as direct conversion. 80% bookings via Instagram CTA cited.
Pillar 5
Industry event presence (HairCon) — booth, panels, "Your Brand" social stage. Positions Fresha as industry advocate.
Tone
Energetic, global, feature-first. Talks to salon owners as entrepreneurs and creatives. Avoids anything that sounds like "platform fees." Heavy use of stats and percentages to signal legitimacy.
Core hook
"World's #1 booking platform" repeated constantly. Aspirational scale + accessibility. LinkedIn = growth narrative for salon professionals. Instagram = B2C inspiration + booking conversion.
What they never say
The "free forever" reversal. The 20% new client fee charged on own-sourced clients. No acknowledgment of the trust crisis in reviews. Social channels are entirely upbeat — the gap between public messaging and actual user experience is the widest of any competitor.
Notino's opening
Fresha's social-to-reality gap is exploitable. Run honest "we don't do that" content — transparent pricing breakdowns, no-hidden-fee narratives. Where Fresha talks scale, Notino talks trust. Real salon owner testimonials with specific €/month savings comparisons.
3. Salonkee
Salonkee
B2B only
Channels: Instagram @salonkee.de.at (4.4k, active Reels) · LinkedIn · Localised salonkee.at site
"Stop wasting hours on admin. Join thousands of salons across Europe using Salonkee to save time and grow faster."
Pillar 1
Time savings / admin relief — "your calendar fills itself", "stop chasing no-shows." Pain-first messaging that resonates immediately.
Pillar 2
Feature education via Reels — short demos of specific features (waitlists, deposits, staff management). Shows rather than tells.
Pillar 3
Customer success stories — named businesses, before/after revenue or efficiency metrics. Trustpilot integration in content.
Pillar 4
0% commission messaging — regularly reinforces no hidden fees. Direct counter to Treatwell and Fresha. Fixed monthly fee framed as predictability.
Tone
Professional, warm, helpful. Talks to salon owners as business operators who deserve good tools. German primary. Feels like a knowledgeable local colleague, not a global tech platform. Support-first culture bleeds into content.
Core hook
"All-in-one ops platform, 0% commission, built for European salons." The hook is reliability + completeness. Not cheap — dependable. Biggest differentiator: predictable costs, full control.
What they never say
Anything about revenue generation for salons — it's purely an efficiency/cost framing. Never talks about bringing new customers. Never mentions product sales or upsell economics. The salon owner is always paying Salonkee, never earning through it.
Notino's opening
Salonkee frames software as a cost of doing business. Notino can flip this: "we don't just save you time, we generate revenue you didn't have." The product commission narrative is completely absent from Salonkee's content — Notino owns this space. Ask: "Where does Salonkee send you new clients?"
4. Planity
Planity
B2B dominantAT: zero today
Channels: LinkedIn (corporate + CEO-led) · press/media heavy · French-primary. German Capterra listings pre-seeded for AT. No AT social presence yet.
"After securing a leadership position in France and quickly establishing presence in Germany and Belgium, we will strive to replicate our national success." — CEO, Series C announcement
Pillar 1
Funding / traction narrative — €50M raise, 50k salons, #1 France. Every piece of external content leads with scale and momentum.
Pillar 2
0% commission as a moral argument — frames commission models as structurally unfair to salons. Not just cheaper — ethically correct.

Pillar 4
AI roadmap — conversational AI for phone booking, payment automation. Positions Planity as forward-looking tech vs older SaaS players.
Tone
Confident, founder-led, slightly evangelical. CEO Antoine Puymirat is the face — personal LinkedIn and press interviews drive more content than corporate channels. Very French startup energy: principled, design-conscious, anti-commission as a values statement.
Core hook
"0% commission SaaS for beauty. No marketplace dependency. Salons keep their clients." In France/Germany/Belgium this resonated quickly because it directly attacked the Treatwell model. In AT it hasn't landed yet — but will.
What they never say
Anything about revenue generation or product upsell for salons. No e-commerce integration, no loyalty ecosystem, no product commission. Content is entirely about booking management and cost savings — not salon revenue growth. No local AT voices or German-speaking salon stories.
Notino's opening
When Planity launches in AT it will own the "0% commission + marketplace" message. Notino must pre-empt NOW: "we've been here for years, we already have 2.4M Austrian users, and we pay you 5% on products." Planity's content is about what salons don't pay — Notino's can be about what salons earn.
5. studiobookr / studiolution
studiobookr / studiolution
B2B onlyAT: weak
Channels: Instagram, TikTok, Facebook, YouTube (Germany-primary). studiobookr Facebook: 275 followers. AT social footprint minimal.
"Du arbeitest nicht auf einer fremden Plattform. Das bedeutet: keine Werbung, keine Empfehlungen für andere Salons in deiner Umgebung und keine Buchungsgebühren. Deine Kundendaten gehören dir." — studiobookr.de
Pillar 1
Data sovereignty / independence — "your client data belongs to you." Strong anti-marketplace, anti-advertising message. Fear of platform dependency.
Pillar 2
Simplicity — the digital equivalent of the paper appointment book, always on. Not overwhelming, not complex. Made for hairdressers.
Pillar 3
No commissions, no hidden costs — repeated constantly. Pricing is the brand. The monthly fee is freedom from percentage-based surprises.
Pillar 4
Industry credibility — founding member of IGZTK, GoBD/KassenSichV compliance. Speaks to German regulatory fluency. Trust via association.
Tone
Calm, no-nonsense, craft-respecting. Addresses Friseure directly — not generic "beauty professionals." Avoids startup energy. Sounds like people who understand the trade, not just tech. TikTok is more playful (behind-the-scenes of the software team).
Core hook
"Your own booking system, not a foreign platform." The anti-Treatwell pitch framed around autonomy and data control — not just cost. Resonates with privacy-conscious German-speaking owners.
What they never say
Anything about bringing new customers, product sales, or revenue growth. The framing is entirely defensive (protect what you have, keep your data, avoid fees). No forward-looking revenue narrative. Very Germany-focused — AT content near zero.
Notino's opening
studiobookr's message is about protection from harm. Notino's should be about opportunity. Address the same privacy concern: "your client data is yours" — but add: "and we bring you new clients through 2.4M Notino users, plus 5% on every product they buy."
What nobody is saying — Notino's content white space
Revenue growth narrative
Every competitor talks about costs, commissions, or admin. Zero competitors talk about making salons meaningfully more money. The 5% product commission story is completely unclaimed in the Austrian market. Notino should own: "Your clients already buy cosmetics online. Why not earn from it?"
The honest comparison post
No competitor publishes a transparent side-by-side cost breakdown. Treatwell and Fresha actively obscure their true costs. A clean "what you actually pay on each platform" carousel — with specific Austrian examples — would be both high-engagement and impossible for competitors to rebut.
Austrian salon owner voice
Salonkee and studiobookr publish DACH testimonials, but few Austrian ones. Planity has none. A content strategy built around named Viennese, Graz, and Salzburg salon owners would be locally unique and credible.
The "Fresha refugee" content
The trust collapse at Fresha is widely documented in public forums. A Notino "Switch from Fresha" campaign — specific, sympathetic, honest — could capture high-intent salon owners right now on Facebook salon owner groups where the complaints are loudest.
Risk: Planity will close some gaps
When Planity launches in AT, they will claim the 0% commission moral high ground and the "anti-Treatwell" voice. Notino needs to establish these narratives before that happens — the window is 6–12 months, which means content investment starts now.
Risk: zero B2B brand awareness
Notino is trusted by Austrian consumers as a cosmetics shop. Salon owners don't yet know Notino as a business partner. The content strategy must introduce the B2B brand AND make the economic case simultaneously — harder than building on an existing B2B reputation.
