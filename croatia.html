Croatia vs Austria — key differences
What's the same
EU member — GDPR, harmonised regulations. Heavily fragmented salon market, 85–95% single-owner operators. Notino brand already trusted by Croatian consumers for cosmetics. Online booking adoption growing fast (Zoyya: up to 80% of reservations online once onboarded). Instagram and Facebook are primary discovery channels.
What makes Croatia better for Notino
No Treatwell. No Booksy. No Planity. Both active platforms (Zoyya + SrediMe) are underfunded regional startups. Digital booking still not fully normalised — Notino can define the category. Tourism creates massive summer demand spikes that no platform is monetising for B2B. English-language tourist booking is entirely uncontested. €170M EU sustainable tourism loans create a structural tailwind for salon and spa investment.
Tourism macro drivers — Croatia's structural advantage
20.2 million tourist arrivals and 93.7 million overnight stays in commercial accommodation in 2024 (DZS data), a 3.9% and 1.4% increase vs 2023. When nautical charters and non-commercial stays are added (eVisitor/eCrew), the Ministry of Tourism summarises 2024 at 21.3 million arrivals and 108.7 million overnight stays, roughly 86% of nights in the Adriatic region and about 2.6 million nights in Zagreb.

Istria alone: 4.9 million arrivals and 27.9 million tourist nights (~24% and 30% of national totals). Dubrovnik: 4.2 million tourist nights (highest single destination).

Tourist origins: Germans (414,400 arrivals, 1.6M nights early in year, +23.6% and +16.5%), Slovenians (341,100 arrivals, 1M nights, +8.4%), followed by Austria, Poland, Czechia, Italy. Health tourism visitors from Western Europe (Germany, UK, Scandinavia).

Implication: Croatia's beauty-services demand is structurally elevated relative to its resident population (3.87M) because of inbound tourism, especially along the coast and in Zagreb. No current platform is capitalising on this for either salon B2B acquisition or tourist consumer booking.
Tourism seasonality — Croatia's unique dynamic
90.7% of overnight stays are from foreign tourists. Coastal cities serve millions of international visitors in summer — beauty services (nails, waxing, hair, aesthetics) are peak demand for tourists and locals alike. Dubrovnik alone: 4.2M tourist nights. Istria: 27.9M. No current platform is capitalising on this for either salon B2B acquisition or tourist consumer booking.

English + German + Italian UX is a direct differentiator — tourists cannot use Zoyya (Croatian-primary) or SrediMe (Serbian-primary) effectively.
Digital booking behaviour
Zoyya case data
Partners see up to 80% of reservations booked online once fully onboarded. Up to 30% more arrivals per customer after adopting online booking. Substantial shares of appointments booked outside working hours (evening/night scheduling). Up to 50% less admin time. Integration with Google Search and Maps ("Book now" button) drives passive discovery.
Market readiness
SrediMe offers 24/7 real-time booking across multiple channels. Croatian consumers already expect on-demand, instant booking regardless of season. Lower digital booking maturity than Austria — phone/WhatsApp still dominates. More per-salon education needed. But adoption accelerates once onboarded (Zoyya data). The gap is awareness, not willingness.
Consumer behaviour insights
Price sensitivity — tourist vs local dynamics
Locals: Very sensitive to explicit booking fees and harsh no-show penalties. Avoid visible booking surcharges. Monetise through product commission and B2B fees instead.

Tourists (especially in 4-5* hotels and high-end coastal salons): Will tolerate 20–50% higher price points than inland locals for convenience, quality, last-minute or Sunday appointments.

Foreign patients/spa guests: Come to Croatia for good value-for-money vs Western Europe but are willing to pay premium relative to local price levels.
No-show patterns & management
Zoyya and SrediMe both highlight automatic reminders (email/SMS) and simple rescheduling as core features to reduce no-shows. Zoyya charges per SMS and offers prepayment features through payment-gateway partners. Some salons already experimenting with deposits and pre-paid bookings.

Strategy: Reminder-driven no-show reduction combined with optional deposits, rather than punitive cancellation fees. Focus on reminders and optional deposits — punitive fees land badly in this price-sensitive market.
Linguistic & cultural specifics
Language expectations by audience: In tourist hubs, English is the main lingua franca; German and Italian widely spoken. Croatian (HR) and English (EN) as default languages for B2C app and salon dashboard. German (DE) and Italian (IT) as priority for coastal and Istrian/Kvarner salons. Optional regional languages (Slovene, Polish) via translation of key static pages.

Cultural norms: Strong culture of regular hairdressing, nail care and spa visits, particularly among women in urban and coastal areas. Wellness services integral to both domestic leisure and inbound health-tourism trips. Beauty services not purely discretionary luxuries — embedded in social and tourism routines.

Salon brand profile: 28.8% Croatian-named salons, 21.5% English-branded, 8.2% Balkan, 7.7% International, 5.6% South Slavic — reflecting a mix of local identity and international aspiration.
Target personas
B2C
Urban Croatian beauty seeker
Female 20–45, Zagreb/Split/Rijeka/Osijek. Instagram-native, increasingly booking digitally. Already shops Notino for cosmetics — loyalty cross-sell is natural.
B2C — tourist
International summer visitor
German, Slovenian, Austrian, Italian visitor May–September. Needs English booking. Can't navigate Croatian/Serbian-primary platforms. Notino's multilingual UX wins here.
B2B — primary ICP
Small urban salon owner
1–3 chairs, Zagreb or secondary city. Phone/WhatsApp booking, paper diary still common. Cost-sensitive. Notino's zero cost + product commission is a no-brainer once explained.
B2B — coastal ICP
Tourist-area salon / spa
Split, Dubrovnik, Hvar, Poreč operators. Overwhelmed July–August, slow November–March. Need tourist acquisition and demand smoothing. Currently invisible to international visitors.
EU funding tailwind
€170M sustainable tourism loans (HBOR + ERDF, Nov 2024): Croatia's government programme funds construction, renovation and equipping of tourism establishments including health, wellness and spa facilities. Individual loans €250k–€7M with up to 50% principal writedown if criteria are met. This creates a direct investment wave in hotel spas, wellness centres and clinic-adjacent beauty facilities — all prime Notino Partner ICP targets with above-average ticket sizes and year-round demand. Notino can include this in its B2B pitch to premium coastal operators investing in upgrades.
Notino's position — Croatia-specific
Where Notino wins outright
Established Notino brand in Croatia — e-shop already active, beauty brand trusted. Consumer base exists; verify exact user count with Notino data team before using externally.
Tourist pipeline nobody else has. Germans, Slovenians, Austrians dominate Croatia's overnight tourism — Notino's existing EU user base already reaches them across the e-shop.
5% product commission — completely unprecedented. Zoyya and SrediMe cost salons money. Notino pays them.
Green field — category creation is still possible. Defining what a beauty booking platform looks like in Croatia has not yet happened.
Croatia-specific challenges
Lower digital booking maturity than Austria. Phone/WhatsApp still dominates. More per-salon education needed.
Seasonal income concentration — coastal salons earn 60–70% of revenue in July–August. Hard sell in winter. Spring is the acquisition window.
SrediMe's verified-review moat takes time to replicate. Prioritise review collection from Notino e-shop customers who visit partner salons from day one.
Smaller absolute market than Austria ($420M vs $2.51B) — lower revenue ceiling, but also dramatically lower competition cost.
Strategic white space
Tourist booking in EN/DE/IT
108.7M overnight stays, 90.7% from foreign tourists. Zero platforms addressing them in their language. Notino's multilingual UX + existing EU user base = a genuine tourist pipeline for coastal salons — not a marketing claim, an actual product advantage.
"We pay you" narrative
Every competitor charges salons. Zoyya: €23/month + €0.50 per payment. SrediMe: subscription. Fresha: 20% commissions. Notino's 5% product commission narrative is completely unclaimed and genuinely disruptive in this market.
April–May coastal acquisition window
Coastal salon owners are most receptive April–May when planning for summer. They want full tourist calendars. The pitch writes itself: "Be visible to German and Austrian tourists before June." Miss this window and you sell into winter survival mode.
EU tourism funding tie-in
€170M in HBOR/ERDF loans for spa/wellness investment creates a wave of premium coastal operators investing in upgrades. Notino can be the natural booking + retail partner for this segment — above-average tickets, year-round demand.
Risk: Zoyya raises Series A
Zoyya is the only player with genuine local credibility. A funding round would accelerate B2B sales, product development and brand visibility. Monitor their funding signals — if they raise, the category-definition window narrows significantly.
Risk: SrediMe deepens HR investment
SrediMe has the verified-review moat and existing Croatian salon relationships. If they invest in Croatian-language marketing and a local B2B push, they become harder to displace. Target Zagreb and Split salons currently on SrediMe first.

Against Zoyya
Emphasise incremental revenue: Notino brings beauty-product sales and loyalty points on top of bookings, while Zoyya focuses on bookings only. Offer migration support and co-branding ("Book via Notino" + "We are on Notino Partner") to reduce perceived switching cost.

Prioritise acquisition of high-value salons in Zagreb and key coastal cities before these platforms invest heavily. Stress total cost of ownership and brand independence: no heavy commissions, ownership of customer relationships, access to Notino's large beauty assortment and marketing engine.

Croatia has two analysable competitors for social content. Zoyya has 417 posts of documented content strategy. SrediMe has an established regional content playbook. Both have significant blind spots that Notino can own entirely. This analysis maps every pillar, identifies every gap, and specifies the exact content moves Notino should make.
Zoyya — social content deep dive
@zoyyaapp · 1,914 IG followers · 1,600 FB followers · 417 posts · Croatian-primary
Messaging pillars
Admin relief
"Stop answering WhatsApp at midnight." Blog posts on no-shows, cancellations, client databases. Reels showing chaos of phone booking vs calm of digital calendar.
Partner success stories
Named Zagreb salon case studies with specific metrics — "80% of bookings now online", "50% less admin time". Featured interviews: "Why successful salons use Zoyya?"
Education first
Heavy blog: salon marketing guides, social media tips, staff management, how to get reviews, client database importance. Positions Zoyya as the smart owner's advisor.
"Support local" community
Croatian startup identity, "first service marketplace in Croatia" pride, community-building. Tone of a local colleague, not a SaaS vendor. Very effective for trust.
Feature demos
Reels demoing calendar view, Google Maps integration, booking links for WhatsApp and Instagram bio. Practical how-to content for tech-hesitant salon owners.
Tone & voice
Warm, locally grounded, educational. Croatian-language primary — feels genuinely local, not translated. Reads like a knowledgeable friend who happens to run a salon software company. Never corporate, never salesy. Uses real salon owner names and stories. The closest analogy: a trusted industry newsletter written by someone who actually knows Croatian salon culture.
Core hook
"Reclaim your time." Every pillar funnels back to this. No-show reduction, admin savings, client reminders — all positioned as time given back to the salon owner. The emotional anchor is relief and control, not growth or revenue. The implicit promise: you became a stylist, not a receptionist. Zoyya handles the receptionist part.
What Zoyya never talks about
Revenue generation
Zoyya never frames itself as a money-making tool. Always operational savings, never "grow your income." Zero product upsell or retail commission angle.
Tourist clients
Not one piece of content addresses the summer tourist opportunity. No "attract German visitors" angle, no multilingual UX mention, no coastal salon case study.
Honest pricing comparison
Never compares their €23/month + payment fees to competitors. Avoids the total cost conversation entirely. The hidden €0.50 + 0.5% fee is never surfaced.
Salary-level income stories
No "this salon owner earns €2k/month more because of Zoyya." Success metrics stay operational (bookings, admin time) not financial (income, revenue).
Seasonal content
No summer tourist prep, no wedding season content, no off-season strategy for coastal owners. The Croatian beauty calendar is completely unaddressed.
Notino's opening vs Zoyya
Zoyya owns "admin relief" completely — don't fight it. Instead, own the revenue narrative they deliberately avoid. Every Zoyya post talks about saving time. Notino talks about making money. The attack surface: "Zoyya fills your calendar. Notino fills your calendar AND pays you 5% on every product your clients buy. And those German tourists who can't figure out Zoyya's Croatian interface? They find you on Notino." The tourist angle is entirely uncontested — 417 Zoyya posts and not one addresses the coastal tourism opportunity. Win with: Croatian salon owner testimonials focused on income not admin. Summer coastal campaign. English-language booking angle.
SrediMe — social content deep dive
@sredimesrbija · Serbia-primary · HR secondary · Active: Zagreb, Split, Rijeka, Dubrovnik
Messaging pillars
Discovery & inspiration
Consumer-facing "find the best salons near you" narrative. Posts about discovering new salons, trying new services, browsing by city. Strong looker-to-booker conversion angle.
Verified trust
100% verified reviews — only from users who actually booked. Most defensible asset. Positioned as "you can trust these reviews" vs general Google/Facebook ratings.
Regional reach
City-launch campaigns (Sarajevo, Novi Sad, Zagreb expansion). Positions as the booking platform for the whole ex-YU region — useful for salons wanting visitors from neighbouring markets.
Salon showcase
Promotional videos for partner salons, before/after service photos, "best salons in [city]" roundup posts. More visual and aspirational than Zoyya — Instagram-native aesthetic.
Choose your stylist
Key feature USP consistently promoted: "Book the person, not just the salon." Specific staff member selection is uniquely surfaced — Zoyya focuses on salon level only.
Tone & voice
Consumer-aspirational, visually led. Instagram-native aesthetic — polished salon imagery, lifestyle photography. Less educational than Zoyya, more inspirational. Serbian-language primary: the branding (@sredimesrbija) signals where home base is. In Croatian cities feels like a visitor's platform rather than a local one. Discovery app energy, not colleague energy.
Core hook
"Find the best, book the best." Consumer-side discovery confidence. Verified reviews tie into this — you're not guessing, you're booking from real people's real experiences. For B2B, the implicit hook: "We bring you clients who already trust us." Less operational than Zoyya and more acquisition-focused — but still no revenue generation story.
What SrediMe never talks about
Revenue or income
Purely a discovery/booking layer. No product commission, no retail tie-in, no income generation content for salon owners at all.
Western tourist market
Positioned for ex-YU visitors (Serbian, Bosnian tourists). Zero content for German, Austrian, Slovenian, Italian visitors — Croatia's highest-spending tourist groups.
Salon management or ops
No blog, no educational content, no case studies. Entirely consumer-facing. B2B salon owners get no value from SrediMe content beyond "we list you."
Loyalty or retention
Strong on acquisition but zero on retention. No loyalty programme, no repeat-visit narrative, no re-engagement content. One-way pipeline only.
Pricing transparency
Never publishes what salons pay to be listed. No pricing comparison, no cost-of-use breakdown for prospective partner salons.
Notino's opening vs SrediMe
SrediMe is a strong discovery layer but a thin partner proposition. The clearest attack is the tourist market split: SrediMe connects Croatian salons to Serbian and Bosnian visitors. Notino connects them to an established base of Croatian beauty shoppers PLUS the Germans, Austrians, and Slovenians who dominate Croatia's overnight tourism — a fundamentally different and higher-spending audience. Content plays: "Meet the clients SrediMe can't bring you." International tourist testimonials. "Why Notino brings you different clients" comparison content. Counter SrediMe's verified review moat with Notino loyalty reviews from existing e-shop customers who visit partner salons — a cross-channel review ecosystem SrediMe can't replicate.
Combined white space — what neither competitor does
Revenue narrative
Neither competitor frames themselves as income generators. Zoyya saves time; SrediMe brings clients. Nobody says "here's how much more you earn." Notino's 5% product commission flips this completely.
"A Split salon earned €340 in product commissions last August — on top of normal service revenue. That's 17 Notino orders from clients they'd already seen in the chair."
Tourist client acquisition
108.7M overnight stays, 90.7% foreign tourists, zero content targeting them. No coastal platform tells salon owners "here's how to attract German tourists in August." Notino's multilingual UX makes this a credible promise — not a claim.
"A Dubrovnik nail salon got 23 bookings from international tourists last July through Notino. They'd never had a tourist find them online before."
Honest "what you're paying now" posts
Neither Zoyya nor SrediMe makes the cost-of-alternatives transparent. A simple breakdown — "Zoyya: €23/month + €0.50 per payment. Notino: €0 if you hit 20 bookings" — is uncontested territory and directly converts their unhappy users.
"What does your booking software actually cost you per month? We did the maths for Croatian salons — the answer surprised us."
Croatian beauty seasonal calendar
No platform publishes Croatian beauty seasonal content — wedding season, summer prep, Christmas, shoulder season. Owning this calendar makes Notino the go-to voice for the Croatian beauty industry rhythm.
"May is Croatia's biggest wedding month. Are your bridal packages showing up where brides search? Here's how Notino Partner salons are filling May calendars right now."
Coastal owner testimonials
Zoyya does Zagreb case studies. SrediMe does urban discovery. Zero content speaks specifically to the Split/Dubrovnik/Poreč salon owner's reality — massive summer, dead winter, desperate for tourist clients.
"Ivana runs a nail salon in Split. In July she's fully booked. In January she counts the days. Notino Partner changed her off-season in ways she didn't expect."
Loyalty cross-sell story
Neither player connects booking to repeat customer revenue. Notino's loyalty loop is a closed circle: client books, gets points, shops on Notino, sees salon products, books again. Nobody is telling this story.
"Your client leaves your salon. She opens Notino, orders the mask you recommended, earns points, and books her next appointment — without you doing anything."
